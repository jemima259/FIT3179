The Trees of Melbourne

An exploration of the trees in Melbourne's CBD to combat urban warming by Jemima Walsh.

Why put trees in a city?

As climate change heats up the planet, this presents new problems to cities such as urban warming. Urban warming refers to the phenomenon where urban surfaces are warmed by the sun and as a result, cities are much warmer than the areas around them, forming “urban heat islands.” These result in consequences such as increased energy consumption, elevated air pollution, and compromised human health. The City of Melbourne’s Urban Forest Strategy aims to mitigate urban warming by improving its urban forest to increase the canopy cover in the city, which will prevent urban warming. As we can see in this map, much of Melbourne already has canopy cover, although there are areas for growth.

Are enough on streets?

Although trees in parks still help to improve air quality and human wellbeing, trees are required in the streets and among the building of cities in order to provide canopy cover for urban surfaces such as roads and rooftops to reduce urban warming. As pictured to the left, most urban trees are in parks, not streets so perhaps tree planting efforts need to focus more on streets.

What was planted when?

In this line graph, we can see that most of the trees that have been planted are in the Myrtaceae family, most of which are Red River Gums (Eucalypts) in Melbourne. Myrtaceae trees are a great choice of tree for an urban forest because they are evergreen, so they won’t lose their leaves and therefore canopy cover in the winter. However, as many on these trees were planted in 1998, in the coming years they will begin to age and become more susceptible to disease such as Myrtle rust which threatens to wipe out the Red River Gums. Therefore other tree types should be considered to increase biodiversity and reduce the impact of a disease outbreak.
